South Korea Calibrated Company Size Thresholds for Six 
Logistics Product Groups 
Executive summary 
This report proposes a simple, Korea-calibrated three-tier size classification for six 
logistics product groups: Fresh_Food, FMCG_Packaged, Pharmaceuticals, 
Industrial_Materials, Durables_Electronics, and Ecommerce_Misc. The classification 
uses annual revenue as the primary criterion and employee count only as a secondary 
guardrail. That choice is deliberate: Korea’s current legal SME framework already relies 
mainly on industry-specific three-year average sales, and since 2015 the formal legal 
test has shifted away from mixed employee/capital rules toward a sales-based standard. 
Korea’s official size system is organized by industry codes, not by logistics product-flow 
groups, so this report maps each product group to the closest Korean legal and statistical 
anchors and then adjusts for logistics realities such as cold chain, regulatory intensity, 
bulk density, SKU velocity, and outsourcing prevalence. [1] 
The proposed large-company thresholds rise in the following order: Fresh_Food KRW 
0.8tn, Pharmaceuticals KRW 1.2tn, FMCG_Packaged KRW 1.5tn, Industrial_Materials 
KRW 2.0tn, Durables_Electronics KRW 2.5tn, and Ecommerce_Misc KRW 3.0tn. The 
logic is operational rather than legal. Fresh food becomes “large” at a lower revenue level 
because cold-chain coordination, spoilage risk, and seasonal peaks create national-
network complexity early. Pharmaceuticals also scale into “large” status relatively early 
because MFDS storage-and-transport controls, especially for biologics, impose 
compliance and temperature-control burdens beyond what revenue alone suggests. By 
contrast, industrial materials have lower SKU complexity and repetitive B2B flows, while e-
commerce firms can postpone owned-infrastructure intensity through 3PL, parcel, and 
fulfillment outsourcing, so their “large” threshold should start later. [2] 
For researchers, the main recommendation is straightforward: use revenue first, then 
employee count only when revenue is unavailable or distorted by pass-through 
trading. For Korean comparability, the revenue bands below are expressed in KRW first 
and translated into USD only for convenience. They are intended as a research 
classification for logistics scale, not as a substitute for Korean statutory SME or 
conglomerate definitions. Korea’s official context remains important: MSME/SME status is 
tied to industry-specific average sales and independence rules, and firms with total assets 
below KRW 500bn may still fail SME tests if they belong to a public disclosure business 
group, a regime that in practice captures much larger conglomerate structures. [3] 
Method and assumptions 
The methodology starts from Korean official size anchors and then applies product-
specific logistics adjustments: 

• Official legal anchors: closest MSS sector ceilings were used where they are 
clearly observable in Korean policy materials, including C10 food manufacturing 
KRW 120bn, C21 pharmaceuticals KRW 80bn, C24 primary metals KRW 180bn, 
and C26 electronics KRW 150bn. These are legal SME context markers, not the 
final thresholds used in this report. [4] 
• Statistical anchors: Statistics Korea online-shopping data, MAFRA cold-chain 
policy materials, MFDS distribution/storage guidance, MOTIE export releases, and 
KOTI logistics research were used to calibrate where logistics complexity 
meaningfully changes by product group. [5] 
• Currency assumption: all USD equivalents use a fixed research conversion of KRW 
1,350 = USD 1. This is a reporting convention for comparability, not a live FX 
forecast. 
• Revenue basis assumption: unless noted otherwise, example companies use the 
latest public full-year figure available, usually FY2025, and usually on a 
consolidated basis when that is how the company reported it. 
• Segment assumption: if a company does not disclose a clean product-group 
segment, it is used only when the firm is widely representative of that logistics 
profile. 
• Private-company assumption: where no clean primary filing snippet was readily 
parseable, I used a company newsroom item or a Korean financial/employment 
database summarizing filed statements; those cases are noted implicitly by the 
source. 
Because Korea’s legal framework is already sales-based, the employee thresholds below 
are best understood as secondary operational guardrails rather than legal tests. When 
revenue and employee count disagree, revenue should govern. [6]

These bands are a synthesis of Korean official size anchors and logistics-specific evidence. 
The most important legal sector anchors are MSS’s sales ceilings for food manufacturing, 
pharmaceuticals, primary metals, and electronics, while the most important 
demand/operations anchors are Kostat’s very large and still-growing online-shopping 
market, MAFRA’s continuing emphasis on low-temperature distribution for fresh 
foods, MFDS biologics transport controls, MOTIE’s large ICT export base, and KOTI’s 
evidence that 3PL expansion can materially change how firms scale logistics 
capacity. [7] 

 
 
Product-group thresholds and representative Korean examples 
Fresh_Food — Small < KRW 80bn, Mid KRW 80bn–800bn, Large ≥ KRW 800bn. This is the 
lowest “large” threshold in the framework because the Korean fresh-food chain is 
unusually sensitive to seasonality, spoilage, and temperature control. MAFRA continues 
to treat low-temperature distribution as a policy priority for fresh agricultural products, and 
Statistics Korea shows ongoing strength in online agricultural/fishery purchases, which 
adds order-splitting and next-day cold-chain pressure. Representative examples are 
Woodeumji Farm as small at roughly KRW 63.7bn of 2025 sales, Sajo Seafood as mid at 
KRW 217.4bn, and CJ Freshway as large at KRW 3.481tn. [8] 
FMCG_Packaged — Small < KRW 120bn, Mid KRW 120bn–1.5tn, Large ≥ KRW 1.5tn. The 
small cutoff is anchored to the Korean legal context for food manufacturing, while the 
higher large cutoff reflects the fact that packaged FMCG is usually ambient, shelf-stable, 
and more compatible with wholesaler and 3PL distribution than fresh food. Korea’s 
online food categories are still growing, but packaged FMCG does not require the same 
universal cold-chain intensity as Fresh_Food, so firms can scale further before their 
logistics footprint becomes structurally “large.” Representative examples are Foodnamoo 
as small at KRW 86.8bn in 2025 sales, Binggrae as mid at KRW 1.4896tn, and Orion as 
large at KRW 3.3324tn. [9] 


Pharmaceuticals — Small < KRW 120bn, Mid KRW 120bn–1.2tn, Large ≥ KRW 1.2tn. 
Korean pharmaceuticals deserve a lower large cutoff than industrial materials or 
electronics even though average volumes are not bulk-heavy, because regulatory 
intensity is much higher. MFDS guidance for biologics explicitly emphasizes storage and 
transport control, and Korea’s official SME ceiling for C21 pharmaceuticals is already 
lower than food or electronics. Add to that the export growth of Korean bio-health 
products and the category becomes logistics-intensive at a relatively modest revenue 
level. Representative examples are CMG Pharmaceutical as small at KRW 105.2bn, 
Dong-A ST as mid at KRW 745.1bn, and Celltrion as large at KRW 4.1625tn. [10] 
Industrial_Materials — Small < KRW 150bn, Mid KRW 150bn–2.0tn, Large ≥ KRW 2.0tn. 
This group gets a higher large threshold because operational complexity here comes more 
from tonnage, plant-warehouse-port coordination, and bulk transport economics than 
from dense SKU management. Korean official SME ceilings for primary metals are high, 
and MOTIE continues to report meaningful steel-export volumes, which is consistent with a 
sector where firms can remain operationally mid-sized even at fairly high revenues if flows 
are repetitive and B2B-direct. Representative examples are Daedong Steel as small at 
KRW 119.2bn, Hanil Cement as mid at KRW 1.4239tn, and Korea Zinc as large at KRW 
16.5879tn. [11] 
Durables_Electronics — Small < KRW 250bn, Mid KRW 250bn–2.5tn, Large ≥ KRW 
2.5tn. I set a relatively high large cutoff because Korean electronics logistics is heavily 
shaped by global sourcing, export orientation, service-parts networks, and reverse 
logistics, but not by the very high order-line fragmentation seen in e-commerce. MOTIE’s 
ICT export data show how internationally scaled this Korean ecosystem is, and MSS’s C26 
legal ceiling is already relatively high. Representative examples are Anam Electronics as 
small at KRW 215.3bn, Cuckoo Homesys as mid at roughly KRW 0.8tn, and LG 
Electronics as large at KRW 89.2tn in 2025 sales. [12] 
Ecommerce_Misc — Small < KRW 100bn, Mid KRW 100bn–3.0tn, Large ≥ KRW 3.0tn. 
Korea’s online-shopping market is already extremely large on a national basis, with 
monthly transaction values above KRW 22tn–24tn and mobile accounting for around 77%. 
Yet I push the large threshold above packaged FMCG because many Korean e-commerce 
firms can scale sales through parcel networks, contracted fulfillment, and 3PL rather 
than owned infrastructure. KOTI explicitly notes that 3PL expansion can be a way to reduce 
logistics costs as e-commerce grows. Representative examples are 09WOMEN / 
Gongguwoman as small at about KRW 45.0bn in 2025 sales, Musinsa as mid at 
approximately KRW 1.47tn in 2025 revenue by inference from official 2024 revenue plus its 
official 2025 sales growth rate, and Coupang as large at USD 34.5bn of 2025 revenue, 
equivalent to roughly KRW 46.6tn under the report’s FX assumption. [13] 
Why the thresholds differ by sector 
The thresholds are not meant to mirror Korean subsidy law; they are meant to capture the 
point at which a Korean company’s logistics system usually moves from regional or 

outsourced coordination into national, compliance-heavy, multi-node management. 
That transition happens earliest in Fresh_Food because temperature excursions, delivery 
timing, and quality loss are immediate commercial risks, and it happens early in 
Pharmaceuticals because MFDS-controlled storage and transport requirements add 
process overhead beyond normal warehousing. [14] 
The transition happens later in FMCG_Packaged because firms can deploy broader 
distributor and wholesaler networks without universal cold chain, and later still in 
Industrial_Materials because low-SKU, repetitive B2B flows make large tonnage 
manageable without the same order-level orchestration. In Durables_Electronics, the key 
complexity is less about SKU velocity than about import content, export routing, warranty 
parts, and service loops. Korea’s export data for ICT and steel help explain why the 
industrial-materials and electronics cutoffs should sit well above fresh food and pharma. 
[15] 
Ecommerce_Misc is the most structurally ambiguous group. Its order-line density, return 
rates, and promotional volatility are logistics-intensive, but Korea’s e-commerce market 
also supports heavy use of outsourced fulfillment and parcel infrastructure. That is why 
the mid range is intentionally wide here and why the revenue threshold for “large” starts 
only when firms are clearly operating at a multi-trillion won, national-platform scale. [16] 
Open questions and limitations 
This framework is rigorous enough for comparative research, but it has three limits. First, 
Fresh_Food and Ecommerce_Misc are not official Korean legal industries; they are 
product-flow groupings mapped onto the closest official anchors, so some judgment is 
unavoidable. Second, a few smaller-company examples rely on summaries of filings or 
business databases where a clean primary-source snippet was not readily parseable; I 
have flagged those implicitly through the cited source type. Third, the thresholds are 
calibrated to South Korea in the 2025–2026 operating context, so they should be re-
checked if Korean inflation, parcel pricing, cold-chain regulation, or e-commerce 
fulfillment structure changes materially. [17]
