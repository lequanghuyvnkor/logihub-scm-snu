Korean Annual Events and Supply Chain Management for 
Six Product Groups 
Executive Summary 
Korea’s logistics calendar is dominated by two culture-led peaks and one retail-led peak. 
The culture-led peaks are Seollal and Chuseok, both lunar-calendar holidays that sharply 
pull forward food, gift-set, parcel, and travel demand. Public Korean survey data show that 
holiday gift buying is still heavily concentrated in the final one to two weeks before the 
holiday: for Seollal, the Rural Development Administration found gift purchases 
concentrated one week before the holiday, and for Chuseok, KAMIS found 45.3% of gift-
set purchases scheduled one week before and 24.4% two weeks before. Chuseok and 
Seollal are officially three-day holidays, though actual breaks often extend when weekends 
or substitute holidays attach; for example, Seollal 2026 falls on February 17 with the 
holiday from February 16 to 18, and Chuseok 2025 fell on October 6 with the holiday 
period from October 5 to 8. [1] 
The retail-led peak is the November mega-sale cluster: Korea Sale Festa, SSG Day, 
Gmarket Big Smile Day, 11Street’s Grand 11th, retailer Black Friday campaigns, and 
Lotte’s late-October/early-November promotions. In official and retailer disclosures, these 
are not one-day spikes but multi-day waves: Korea Sale Festa 2024 ran November 9–30, 
with some appliance and auto discounts starting November 1; Gmarket Big Smile Day 
2024 ran November 1–10; 11Street’s Grand 11th has historically centered on 
November 1–11; Lotte’s Super L.Day 2024 ran October 28–November 6; and Coupang’s 
2024 Black Friday electronics promotion ran to November 17. This matters operationally 
because Korean firms do not prepare for “the day”; they prepare for an extended peak 
corridor with staggered promotion waves, coupon releases, and delivery cutoffs. [2] 
The strongest public quantitative demand evidence is available for fresh food, packaged 
FMCG, durables/electronics, and broad e-commerce through Statistics Korea monthly 
category data and retailer event disclosures. It is far weaker for pharmaceuticals and 
industrial materials, where official releases focus more on continuity of service, 
regulated transport, holiday medical access, 24-hour customs clearance, and port 
operations than on publicly released event-by-event demand percentages. Accordingly, 
the most reliable supply-chain playbook difference is this: consumer categories optimize 
for promotion-driven throughput, while pharmaceuticals and industrial materials 
optimize for service continuity and compliance during holiday disruption. [3]

Product Group Playbooks 
In the playbooks below, T-2 means the early build window, usually around 4–8 weeks 
before the event for consumer goods and sometimes longer for imported or regulated 
items. T-1 is the final 1–2 weeks before the event. T0 is the event/holiday week itself. T+1 is 
the first 1–2 weeks after the event, when returns, settlement, restocking, and correction of 
overbuild dominate. 
Fresh_Food 
Representative public demand evidence is strong and very Korean-specific. E-Mart 
reported that fresh Seollal gift-set preorder sales for 2026 were 28% higher YoY, with 
especially strong growth in pork gift sets (+59%) and one mixed-fruit holiday gift item 
(+142%). Holiday buying is also visibly late: RDA found Seollal gift buying concentrated one 
week before the holiday, while KAMIS found that Chuseok gift-set buying clustered one 
week before (45.3%) and two weeks before (24.4%), with another official KAMIS release 
showing 26.8% buying around the start of the break. [42] 
At T-2, fresh-food firms typically secure upstream supply first and transport second. The 
reason is visible in public Korean practice: retailers expand purchase quantities for proven 
gift lines, government and aT holiday stabilization programs enlarge supply of key “holiday 
items,” and store-backed delivery networks prepare chilled and frozen handling capacity. 
Inventory policy is therefore selective pre-build, not blanket stockpiling: firms raise safety 
stock on top fruit, meat, seafood, and gift-box SKUs; they pre-kit gift sets; and they reserve 
chilled packaging, dry ice, gel packs, and vehicle slots early. By T-1, the focus moves to 
slotting the fastest movers near dispatch points, final supplier confirmations, and 
protecting cold-chain routing integrity. [43] 
At T0, operational control matters more than further discounting. Public Korea-wide fresh-
food T0 percentages are limited, so firms manage by service level rather than by a single 
demand coefficient. The playbook is to enforce order cutoffs, prioritize high-density routes, 
pre-authorize substitutions on volatile perishables, and use same-day or dawn delivery 
networks where possible. SSG’s public logistics disclosures are a good proxy for how 
leading Korean firms do this: daytime deliveries can be fulfilled through 100-plus E-Mart 
backroom logistics facilities, while dawn delivery is offered in major metro regions. For 
event-day specials, firms also stage large one-day volumes in advance; SSG’s 2020 SSG 
Day red king crab event, for example, involved 15 tons for just one day. [44] 
At T+1, the problem flips from stockout to spoilage and residual inventory. RDA’s Seollal 
survey says agri-food purchasing temporarily declines after the holiday and typically 
recovers within 6–10 days, so companies usually reduce inbound purchase orders quickly, 
execute markdowns on residual perishables, transfer inventory across stores or fulfillment 
nodes, and intensify temperature and damage claims review. For fresh food, the main 
post-event risks are not merely “low demand”; they are shrink, expiration, and claims. 
[45] 

FMCG_Packaged 
Published Korean evidence shows strong but less fragile event elasticity than fresh food. E-
Mart disclosed that its “Naturalism” processed gift sets rose 72.4% in Seollal 2025 and 
41.7% in Chuseok 2025. In official Statistics Korea data, online Food & beverages rose 
6.1% month on month in November 2025, while cosmetics rose 7.5% month on month 
as the November sale corridor intensified. [29] 
At T-2, FMCG companies usually pre-build faster and wider than fresh-food firms because 
shelf-life and handling constraints are easier. Inventory strategy is typically a combination 
of promo-specific pre-build, bundle creation, and selective safety stock on hero SKUs 
rather than a broad increase across the whole catalog. Procurement teams lock in brand 
funding, on-pack materials, coupon mechanics, and display quantities earlier than in 
regular weeks. This is visible in Korean retailer campaigns that aggregate large branded 
assortments well ahead of the event, such as Coupang’s food Black Friday, which 
assembled 9,000 items from 1,000 brands, and SSG’s year-end grocery events that 
combine packaged foods and household staples into one broad promotional lane. [46] 
At T0, packaged FMCG runs behave like throughput problems. Transportation becomes 
less about cold-chain sensitivity and more about case-pick efficiency, drop density, and 
last-mile capacity for bulky but nonfragile orders such as water, tissue, detergents, 
snacks, and value packs. SSG’s disclosures again show the Korean pattern: many orders 
are routed through store-backed logistics facilities for same-day coverage, while other 
platforms emphasize Rocket Delivery or guaranteed next-day delivery. Pricing is the main 
demand lever here; coupon stacking, card discounts, and “1+1” style mechanics shape 
the order curve more than any change in intrinsic need. [32] 
At T+1, FMCG cools off more gradually than durables. In official data, online Food & 
beverages in December 2025 was still up 1.1% from November, but that was materially 
softer than the 6.1% MoM jump seen in November. The practical implication is that firms 
usually do controlled replenishment deceleration rather than a full stop: they unwind 
bundles, reverse temporary slotting, reconcile trade-spend accruals with suppliers, and 
move from peak wave-picking back to normal replenishment. The core risks are 
promotion-created overhang in slower SKUs, price-comparison backlash, and inventory 
imbalance across nodes, not severe obsolescence. [47] 
Pharmaceuticals 
For pharmaceuticals, I did not find robust public Korean percentages for demand change 
specifically around annual shopping festivals or holiday weeks that are comparable to the 
consumer-retail data above. What official Korean sources emphasize instead is holiday 
medical access, regulated storage and transport, and continuity planning. For Seollal 
2025, the Ministry of Health and Welfare said that an average of 16,815 medical 
institutions would be designated as open per day during the holiday period, and MOHW 
continues to guide citizens to open hospitals and pharmacies via E-Gen and related 

services. MFDS separately maintains detailed guidance on biologics storage and transport. 
[33] 
At T-2, pharmaceutical firms and wholesalers typically prepare by securing critical 
medicine coverage, validating cold-chain devices for biologics and other temperature-
sensitive products, confirming holiday operating rosters with hospitals and pharmacies, 
and tightening batch-level visibility. Unlike FMCG, the inventory strategy is usually service-
level driven rather than promotion-driven: safety stock is concentrated on critical and 
shortage-prone SKUs, while near-expiry exposure is held down through tighter FEFO 
discipline. Procurement coordination often focuses on supplier confirmation for critical 
APIs or finished goods and on which distribution points will remain operational through the 
holiday. [48] 
At T0, the operating goal is continuity, not promotion. Firms prioritize deliveries to 
hospitals, emergency sites, open pharmacies, and high-dependency patients, while 
maintaining temperature records and transport validation for regulated products. The 
Korean holiday context reinforces this approach: public policy is built around ensuring 
access to care and medicine distribution during the closure period. Transportation 
adjustments therefore mean exception handling, validated temp-controlled vehicles, 
and clear handoff procedures, not aggressive discount-led volume pushes. [33] 
At T+1, companies replenish depleted emergency stock, close temporary allocations, 
reconcile transport logs and temperature records, and review shortage or back-order 
signals rather than chasing a “post-promo” normalization pattern. The main risks are 
regulatory non-compliance, cold-chain deviations, and delayed refill demand after holiday 
closures. Public Korea-wide event-period percentage changes remain unavailable, so a 
prudent logistics model should treat pharmaceuticals as a continuity-and-compliance 
category, not a holiday-promotion category. [49] 
Industrial_Materials 
For industrial materials, publicly released Korean data are also sparse on event-period 
demand percentages. Official releases instead show how firms and agencies manage 
around holiday disruption. Korea Customs’ Seollal 2026 special measures ran from 
February 2 to 18, offering 24-hour special customs clearance through 34 customs 
offices for holiday goods and urgent imported raw materials, while the Busan Port 
Authority’s Chuseok playbooks show that port operations are maintained at near-normal 
levels on most holiday days, with emergency cargo handled even on the holiday itself and 
with 24-hour Port-MIS standby and emergency response. [34] 
At T-2, industrial-materials firms usually relax pure JIT behavior and temporarily build 
buffer stock on imported inputs, export cargo, or line-critical spares. Procurement 
coordination focuses on supplier shipment timing, customs paperwork readiness, and the 
arrival sequencing of inbound raw materials. If a plant or customer network will close for 
Seollal or Chuseok, firms often front-load shipments into the last working window before 
the break. Official customs support for urgent raw materials strongly suggests that this is a 

recurring system-wide need in Korea, even if national demand percentages are not 
published. [50] 
At T0, the operating question is which flows must continue despite factory shutdowns and 
reduced staffing. Transportation strategy typically shifts to priority dispatch, near-port 
staging, and exception management for cargo that must clear during the holiday. Busan 
Port’s special operation plans explicitly keep emergency cargo moving and maintain 24-
hour information and support systems, which is exactly the type of infrastructure industrial 
shippers depend on. Warehousing strategy usually becomes simpler: hold more on hand, 
move less frequently, and avoid unnecessary line-side stockouts caused by the holiday 
break. [51] 
At T+1, the system faces a restart problem. Firms release temporary buffers, clear 
customs and inbound backlog, stagger production ramps, and restore tighter 
replenishment logic only after port, trucking, and customer receiving patterns normalize. 
The biggest post-event risks are a synchronized restart that creates short-lived congestion, 
paperwork bottlenecks, and mismatch between inbound raw materials and resumed 
production. Because public event-specific demand percentages are not published, 
industrial-materials planning should be driven by calendar shutdown risk, customs 
support windows, and port operating plans rather than by retail-style demand uplift 
assumptions. [34] 
Durables_Electronics 
Durables and electronics show some of the clearest public event-driven demand swings in 
Korea. Statistics Korea reported that online Home electric appliances rose 12.4% month 
on month in November 2025, while computers and computer-related appliances rose 
28.1% month on month. On the retailer side, Gmarket reported that in the first week of Big 
Smile Day 2023, appliance GMV rose 73%, monitors 136%, audio devices 72%, and high-
ticket categories overall 68%. After the peak, official data showed that online Home 
electric appliances fell 7.2% month on month in December 2025, with home electric 
appliances and electronic equipment down 9.7% from November. [52] 
At T-2, companies typically lock in promotional allotments with brand principals and 
reserve delivery or installation capacity for heavy or high-service products. Korea Sale 
Festa 2024 is a good example of why this must start early: appliance discounts began 
November 1, before the formal festival opening on November 9. Inventory strategy is 
usually pre-build plus prioritized allocation: finished goods and accessories are staged 
in advance, while lower-volume models are often left on tighter replenishment. 
Procurement and supplier coordination are most intense on flagship SKUs, bundle 
components, and installation-related materials. [53] 
At T0, the decisive advantage is fulfillment speed. Coupang’s 2024 Black Friday electronics 
event was built on Rocket Delivery, free shipping, next-day arrival, and free returns across 
10,000-plus appliances and digital products; Gmarket’s 2026 Big Smile Day emphasized 
that about 30% of its core “hero deal” items would move through Star Delivery for next-

day arrival. That tells you what Korean firms actually optimize: fast-ship hero SKUs, dense 
parcel routing, and low-friction returns. Warehouses typically move to peak wave picking, 
tighter slotting for high-volume items, and dedicated handling lanes for bulky electronics. 
[37] 
At T+1, electronics cool off sharply and reverse logistics becomes central. The official -
7.2% MoM decline in total home electric appliances from November to December 2025 
supports the familiar post-peak pattern. Companies usually cut replenishment on promo 
SKUs, pull back on installation slot reservations, and move labor toward returns 
inspection, refurbishment, warranty handling, and customer-service backlog. Pricing also 
shifts: the strongest event coupons disappear, and firms decide whether to preserve list-
price integrity or continue thinner “tail discounts” to clear residual stock. The operational 
risk is not only lower demand; it is return-heavy demand. [54] 
Ecommerce_Misc 
This broad group covers the long tail of online marketplace demand: household goods, 
lifestyle products, small general merchandise, and mixed-category marketplace orders. 
Here the public Korean demand signals are dramatic. Gmarket says Big Smile Day delivers 
participating sellers average sales increases of more than 5x, or +437%, versus normal 
periods. It also said its Seol/Chuseok “Big Sale” campaigns produce more than 3x 
average sales (+206%) for participating sellers. At the platform level, official total online 
shopping still shows the same pattern in softer form: +5.7% month on month in 
November 2025, then -2.0% month on month in January 2026 after the year-end season. 
Shinsegae also reported SSG Day 2024 sales above KRW 2 trillion, up 20% YoY, while its 
spring Landers Shopping Festa 2025 sales rose 14%. [55] 
At T-2, marketplace operators and sellers focus on assortment, stock placement, and 
channel enrollment more than on manufacturing lead times. Sellers bring inventory into 
3PLs or marketplace fulfillment, enroll high-probability SKUs into guaranteed-delivery 
lanes, prepare promo pages and coupons, and add customer-support staffing. The 
11Street and Gmarket disclosures show what serious prep looks like in Korea: tens of 
thousands of sellers, pre-alert campaigns, pre-reservation mechanics, and fulfillment 
products such as Shooting Delivery and Star Delivery are mobilized before the main wave 
starts. [56] 
At T0, throughput spikes can be extreme. Gmarket’s first day of Big Smile Day 2023 
delivered +46% YoY GMV, and 11Street’s “10-minute rush” generated +438% November 
GMV versus October. Gmarket also pushed about 30% of core hero deals through Star 
Delivery for next-day arrival, while 11Street said its Shooting Delivery GMV during Grand 
11th had risen more than 15x in an earlier November peak. The immediate supply-chain 
implications are temporary parcel-capacity additions, stricter order throttling, faster seller 
settlement expectations, and more aggressive routing and cut-off management. [57] 
At T+1, marketplace operations quickly become a reverse-logistics and settlement 
problem. Official total online-shopping value falling 2.0% month on month in January 

2026 after December captures the post-peak normalization at macro level. In practice, 
firms wind down temporary labor, process returns and exchanges, reconcile seller 
payments and chargebacks, and decide which SKUs remain in fast-delivery programs. The 
biggest risks are overselling, customer-service congestion, and financially inefficient 
inventory left in fulfillment nodes after the promotion window closes. [58] 
Source and Method Notes 
This report intentionally prioritizes primary Korean public sources: Statistics Korea / 
Ministry of Data and Statistics for category-level online transaction values, the Ministry of 
Health and Welfare for holiday medical continuity, the Ministry of Food and Drug Safety for 
storage and transport guidance, Korea Customs Service for holiday customs support, 
Busan Port Authority for port operating plans, Korea Tourism Organization for holiday 
timing, and retailer official newsrooms for event windows and campaign effects. Citations 
are the source links. [59] 
The demand percentages in this report are therefore directional and mixed-format by 
necessity. Some are official MoM/YoY transaction-value changes by category; others 
are retailer-reported GMV or sales uplifts during named events. They are useful for 
logistics sizing and relative peak identification, but they are not strictly apples-to-apples 
volume measures. Where public Korean quantitative demand-change data was not 
available—most notably for Pharmaceuticals and Industrial_Materials—the report 
states that explicitly rather than inventing a proxy percentage. [60]
